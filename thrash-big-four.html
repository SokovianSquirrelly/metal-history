Thrash & The Big Four

Thrash metal is defined by its more extreme take on heavy metal, being often faster and more aggresive than most conventional heavy metal at the time. Common elements of the genre include fast, percussive beats, a shredding lead guitar, and low-register guitar riffs. Lyrical themes were often critical about politics, environmental ruin, and occasionally Christianity. In a lot of ways, thrash metal lyrical themes were similar to those of hardcore punk. In fact, it's not wrong to say that thrash is a light fusion between hardcore punk and British heavy metal at the time. The philosophy of the genre was born out of a rejection of Reagan-era conservative views as well as the more pop-inspired metal subgenre known as glam metal.

This era spawned the "Big Four", which is a term given to Metallica, Megadeth, Slayer, and Anthrax. These bands were the most influential and commercially successful in the genre. In fact, as of 2022, Metallica is the most commercially successful band across all of heavy metal of all time. These bands helped to define and build the genre of thrash metal.

The thrash metal scene spawned most prominently in three distinct regions. Bands like Metallica, Megadeth, and Testament came from the San Francisco Bay Area and had a more progressive and technical sound to their music. On the East Coast, especially in New York, bands like Anthrax, Nuclear Assault, and Overkill had a more punk-like sound which inspired Crossover Thrash. And in Europe, bands like Kreator, Destruction, and Sodom hailed from Germany and borrowed elements from both West Coast and East Coast thrash while often employing death metal vocals to create Teutonic thrash metal. Oh, and I would be remiss if I didn't mention Sepultura, who hailed from Brazil but who's sound closely resembles the German thrash scene.

Thrash metal also inspired what has become known as the "battle vest". A battle vest is another name for a sleeveless denim vest with sewn-on patches of various bands on it. This has become a cultural piece of the larger metal scene.

By the mid-90s, the genre had declined in popularity in favor of alternative metal, grunge, and nu metal. However, it was instrumental in developing other heavier subgenres like death metal, black metal, and groove metal.

Music Example: Metallica - Master of Puppets

Even though by 1990, thrash metal had largely faded from mainstream popularity, it did directly influence groove metal. The first groove metal album would come from the Texas-based heavy metal band known as Pantera, and the album was known as "Cowboy's from Hell", which gave a new style of metal evolved from thrash metal while incorporating raspy vocals, down-tuned guitars, heavy riffs, and syncopated rhythms. Unlike its fast-paced parent, groove metal tends to have a slower tempo than thrash. Aside from Pantera, the genre was also popularized by White Zombie and Machine Head. Today, some of the more notable groove metal bands include Lamb of God, DevilDriver, and five Finger Death Punch.

Music Example: Pantera - Walk
