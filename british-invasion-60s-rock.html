British Invasion of '60s Rock

In the mid-1960s, rock bands from the United Kingdom started gaining traction with their takes on rock n' roll. The sound was heavier and more raw than the rock n' roll music in America. Much of this music was considered "hard rock" or just simply "rock". This music perpetuated the rebellious nature of early rock n' roll and its popularity with teenagers at the time. With this music gaining popularity with American audiences, America began its own scene of harder music as well. Eventually, this inspired the psycheleia subculture, or "hippie" subculture known for its use of psychedelic drugs such as LSD and cannabis.

The subject matter of music in this era tended to vary due to some of the events happening at the time. Due to the psychedelic subculture, bands like the Beatles and the Rolling Stones wrote music about this culture and the drugs involved in the subculture. This subject matter carried over into the '70s with bands like Pink Floyd and eventually inspired stoner rock and stoner metal.

One sub-genre at the time that was important in the Civil Rights movement was motown. Motown was a fusion of R&B and 60s rock popular among African-American musicians like the Jackson 5, Aretha Franklin, Marvin Gaye, Smokey Robinson, and Stevie Wonder.

The '60s also saw protest music that was written in response to news events at the time. Civil rights was a subject that brought forward protest music, but it became increasingly popular during the time of the Vietnam War and its accompanying draft. This subject matter was widespread across many bands at the time, though there weren't many bands who were specifically "protest bands". It was mostly an issue many bands talked about without focusing all of their music on it.

Other notable musicians at this time were The Doors, Jimi Hendrix, Bob Dylan, The Who, Jefferson Airplane, and Led Zeppelin.
