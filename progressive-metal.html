Progressive Metal &...Djent?

Back in the late-60s to early-70s, progressive rock was formed as a broad genre of rock music being experimented with and combined with other music genres such as classical, jazz, or folk. The purpose was less about making music to dance to and more about making music that was technically impressive. Progressive metal is a fusion of progressive rock and heavy metal. It's well known for being a showcase of technical and experimental skills, using complex rhythms, unorthodox melodies, intense syncopation, and frequent stylistic changes within one song.

Later on, the genre became more diversified by bands such as Between the Buried and Me, Opeth, and Meshuggah combining jazz fusion with death metal, Devin Townsend using ambient sounds with heavy metal, and King Goat, Sierra, and Below the Sun using doom metal with general progressive metal.

Music Example: Dream Theater - The Count of Tuscany

Progressive metal also spawned a subgenre known as djent inspired by the hard-hitting guitar sound of Meshuggah, who are often considered the godfathers of djent. Djent is often characterized by guitar riffs being hard-hitting, palm-muted, and syncopated, which is where the subgenre gets its name, which is an onomatopoeia of those riffs. Guitars also often had an extended range by employing guitars with up to nine strings.

Djent is fairly divisive genre with some musicians not recognizing it as a genre at all, rather that it was just a short-lived stylistic fad of progressive metal. Even members of Meshuggah, who again, are considered godfathers of the genre, have distanced themselves away from the term "djent" since they didn't coin the term and they felt it was a whole genre created solely based on lead guitarist Fredrik Thordendal's playing style. Djent has also been the subject of memes due to musicians and critics finding it corny or silly, despite djent bands like Tesseract, Animals as Leaders and Periphery receiving praise from critics.
