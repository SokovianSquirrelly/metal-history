Origins of Punk

While rock music started out being rebellious in nature, by the mid-70s, it had already become mainstream and its rebellious nature had largely faded. Some musicians who wanted to preserve the rebellious atmosphere of rock n' roll rejected the contemporaries of the genre and shifted into an unconventional and harsher tone. This shift was what started punk rock. Songs were typically short and fast-paced with hard-edged melodies and styles. Lyrics were often shouted and were often about politics (or the distaste thereof) and anti-establishment. The genre embraces a DIY style and early punk bands produced their own music without the oversight or assistance from third-party producers or record labels. Early punk rock bands include Ramones, the Sex Pistols, the Clash, and Patti Smith. More recent punk rock bands include Green Day, Social Distortion, the Offspring, and NOFX.

Music Example: Ramones - Blitzkrieg Bop

Punk rock evolved alongside heavy metal since its inception, giving us their own subgenres. One of the earliest subgenres of punk was hardcore punk, which started in the late-70s in Southern California. Much like its parent genre of punk rock, its lyrics are critical of "the establishment", politics, commercialism, and anything that was part of the mainstream. These lyrics were purposefully confrontational and abrasive. What made this genre "hardcore" was that it was louder, heavier, faster, and harder-hitting. This is some of the earliest music that had some semblance of "harsh vocals" as the vocals were typically shouted, screamed, or chanted. Hardcore punk culture brought upon "moshing", which has become a main staple in punk and metal during live shows in which participants would push each other or slam into each other. Notable hardcore punk bands include Black Flag, Minor Threat, and Circle Jerks.

Music Example: Black Flag - Rise Above

On the other side of the punk spectrum was pop punk. While hardcore punk went harder and faster, pop punk was where one could argue punk decided to go back into the mainstream, using elements of pop into punk rock. This was largely started by Ramones, though the Undertones and Buzzcocks were also early pop punk bands. Throughout the next few decades, pop punk also pulled in elements from new wave, sca, boy band, and hip hop. Pop punk became most popular in the late-90s and early-2000s with bands like Blink-182, Sum 41, New Found Glory, Good Charlotte, Avril Lavigne, and Simple Plan. During the mid-2000s and into the 2010s, many pop punk bands were popular with the "emo" and "scene" crowds, such as My Chemical Romance, Fall Out Boy, Paramore, and All Time Low.

Music Example: Blink-182 - Aliens Exist

While punk is not part of the heavy metal family, it largely has influence within the genre. Some prominent heavy metal subgenres heavily borrow from punk rock. The British heavy metal bands of the early-80s redefined heavy metal by combining the heavy sound of Black Sabbath with the speed and intensity of punk rock. Thrash metal borrowed lyrical themes from the genre shortly thereafter. Metalcore was based off of hardcore punk and perpetuated a similar rebellious nature with the "scene" crowd of the 2000s. It's also difficult today to be exclusively in the metal community and the punk rock community since the two genres are intertwined at this point.
