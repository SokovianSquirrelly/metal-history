The Brutality of Death Metal

Death metal is an extreme subgenre of heavy metal, typically with heavily distorted and low-tuned guitars, aggressive drumming techniques such as double kicks and blast beats, palm muting and tremolo picking, aggressive, growling vocals, and often a minor, atonal, or chromatic chord progression. Lyrics were inspired by many different sources, ranging from religion to political conflict to slasher film violence.

Death metal started in response to thrash metal and early black metal in the early to mid-80s. Bands like Slayer and Venom were large influences on the death metal scene that started in America. This was a widespread movement across America, but a strong death metal scene spawned in Florida thanks to bands like Death, Obituary, Deicide, and Morbid Angel. Other notable early death metal bands include Possessed, Necrophagia, Cannibal Corpse, and Autopsy.

Even though death metal's influence was rapidly spreading, media attention to the new genre quickly moved to the controversial Norwegian black metal scene, which left death metal as an underground genre at the time, especially in America. However, the early death metal scene went on to transform the music scene forever and have a huge impact on pop culture in the 21st century.

Music Example: Death - Born Dead

Even though the genre was relatively underground in America, its influence found solid footing in Europe, especially in Sweden. Sweden developed its own death metal scene with bands like Grave, Carnage, God Macabre, and Entombed. While much of American death metal was generally more abrasive and brutal, Swedish death metal, while still very heavy, was more approachable. This new scene became gateway for melodic death metal, which combined the brutality that was American death metal with melodic or harmonized guitar riffs and sometimes higher-pitched harsh vocals. This opened up what was known as the "Gothenburg Metal Scene" since Gothenburg was where many of these melodic death, or "melodeath" bands originated such as In Flames, At the Gates, and Dark Tranquility. The melodeath scene spread across Europe and even into America in the late-90s and early-2000s with Swedish bands like Arch Enemy (my personal favorite) and Amon Amarth, Finnish bands like Children of Bodom and Insomnium, and American bands like The Black Dahlia Murder. This scene also went to inspire the rise of melodic metalcore in the 2000s, bringing metalcore bands like Killswitch Engage and As I Lay Dying into the mainstream.

Music Example: Arch Enemy - The Eagle Flies Alone

Another popular splinter from death metal was technical death metal, nicknamed "tech-death". Technical death metal focused more on challenging musicianship and complex songwriting. Much of the inspiration for this music came from an unlikely source: jazz fusion. Complex rhythms, odd time signatures, and heavy use of advanced music theory were a staple of this music. Death would be one of the bands to start this new subgenre of death metal, along with Pestilence, Atheist, Cynic, and Nocturnus.

This rise of technical death metal would evolve alongside progressive metal, and the line between death metal and progressive metal often became blurred, which led to bands like Meshuggah and Opeth. Today, one of the biggest technical death metal bands is Archspire.

Music Example: Atheist - Unholy War

On the opposite side from melodic death metal and technical death metal is brutal death metal. Brutal death metal puts more emphasis on the abrasiveness and speed of its music, often at the cost of melody and technical elements. Some of the most notable brutal death metal bands include Suffocation, Cattle Decapitation, Cryptopsy.

Brutal death metal also led to a microgenre called slam death metal, or often just simply known as "slam". Slam is a more niche genre, but it is often the butt of jokes in the metal community, so I figured I'd share. Slam takes brutal death metal and combines it with elements of hardcore punk and hip hop. Unlike most of death metal, guitar solos and blastbeats are all but non-existent. Instead, it employs mid-tempo rhythms, breakdowns, and palm-muted riffing inspired from Suffocation's song, "Liege of Inveracity". Notable slam bands include Devourment and Cephalotripsy.

Music Example: Suffocation - Liege of Inveracity
